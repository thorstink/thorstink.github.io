Modeling the resolution of your observer

Sensors are a robot's equivalent of an eye and are used to perceive the environment. Observations are almost always limited by a resolution. This resolution can originate from the hardware, like the size of a pixel determines the resolution of an image. But it can also be the result of data processing, such as the binning that happens in a fast fourier transform. This _resolution_ also appears in other sciences: in a questionaire for instance when someone is asked to rate their happiness on a discrete scale from one to five.

In my field (_robotics!_) we add (binned) sensor-measurements to a maximum-likelihood estimation problem and often ignore the resolution of the measurement in the observation model. A lot of times that is probably the right thing to do. Such as the case where the resolution is a significatnyl smaller compared to the standard deviation. However, recently I encountered some situations where I thought this resolution thing was jeapordizing my maximum-likelihood estimation. And this post is about the research that I put into this phenonema.

Tooling that I use in this post are my robotics tools (and interests), and likely deviate from the typical tooling that you would see in blog-post about statistics (or maybe it is more about modeling..). I will however try to keep the results clear and seperated from the implementation. But in the same time I will also tell some things about the tooling because I am big fan of a particular library and its related concepts.

A tri-/multi-lateration example

True range multilateration is a common topic in robotics. If we take the scenario where we have a fixed station at an unknown location, and a mobile robot that moves around to and gather range measurements to this station, we have a great case the apply some multilateration sweetness to find the location of this station. In theory we only need three range measurements to determine the locatin of the station. However, if the robot follows a straight path, one can not know where the station is as there are two hypopthesis. But if our robot moves in a something less then straight trajectory, we will be able to determine the station's coordinate (which, for in this post we will keep in two dimensions).
